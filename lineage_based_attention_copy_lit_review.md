# Has the "Attention-Copy Mechanism" Been Reported Before? A Literature Assessment of Three Sub-Claims

## TL;DR
- **The general phenomenon is well-precedented, but the specific packaging is partly novel.** All three sub-claims have substantial prior art: the "late reader head that copies a referenced token to the output slot" is essentially a name-mover/attribute-extraction head — and the specific head, gemma-2-2b L18.H5, was already named as an "attribute extraction head" by Ferrando, Obeso, Rajamanoharan & Nanda ("Do I Know This Entity?", arXiv:2411.14257, ICLR 2025); the early-establish/late-read two-stage structure closely matches the established factual-recall pipeline (Geva et al. 2023) and the canonical induction-head template; and "sycophancy as a distributed strategy, not one circuit" is directly anticipated by Vennemeyer, Duong, Zhan & Jiang ("Sycophancy Is Not One Thing", arXiv:2509.21305) and the prompt-specific-circuits literature.
- **The report's headline novelty claim — that this is a NEW kind of head distinct from IOI name-movers because the bias is "established early and read late" — does not hold up well.** That early-write/late-read division of labor is exactly the canonical S-inhibition→name-mover and previous-token→induction-head compositional structure, and the "early MLP enrichment, late attention extraction" structure of Geva et al. (2023). The genuinely novel contribution is narrower: the specific head-level routing dichotomy (salience-framing → concentrated L18.H5 vs. numeric-assertion → diffuse mid-stack heads) in gemma-2-2b, which no located source reports exactly.
- **The thinnest, most defensible-as-novel axis is the cue-dependent head routing of a copy strategy.** The constituent ideas — prompt-specific circuits (Franco et al., arXiv:2602.13483: "there is no single circuit for IOI in any model"); sycophancy = many mechanisms (Vennemeyer et al.); arithmetic = "bag of heuristics" (Nikankin, Reusch, Mueller & Belinkov, arXiv:2410.21272, ICLR 2025) — are each established, but their combination into a specific salience-vs-numeric head-routing contrast on gemma-2-2b L18.H5 appears not to have been published.

## Key Findings

**Sub-claim (1): "salience-anchored late reader head that copies a referenced prompt token to the output slot" — HEAVILY PRECEDENTED, and the specific head is already named in prior work.**
- This is the defining behavior of a **name-mover head** (Wang et al. 2023, "Interpretability in the Wild"): heads active at the END token that "attend to previous names in the sentence, copy the name they attend to, and send it to the output." The salience-reader head is a name-mover head pointed at a distractor instead of the indirect object.
- More strikingly, **the exact head — gemma-2-2b L18.H5 — was already identified and named by Ferrando, Obeso, Rajamanoharan & Nanda (2024, "Do I Know This Entity?", arXiv:2411.14257, ICLR 2025)** as one of the model's "attribute extraction heads." Their Appendix L (Table 7) explicitly names "attribute extraction heads L18H5 and L20H3 in Gemma 2 2B" and gives concrete examples — e.g., L18H5 mapping "Kawhi Leonard → Clippers, Niagara, Raptors" — and the paper states that entity-recognition latents "disrupt the attention of downstream heads that typically move entity attributes to the final token." So the claim that L18.H5 is a generalising late reader head that copies a referenced entity token to the answer position is, at the head level, a re-identification of a head already in the literature, not a new discovery.
- Token-level copying via the OV circuit at a late "readout" position also traces to induction heads (Olsson et al. 2022) and the original transformer-circuits framework (Elhage et al. 2021).

**Sub-claim (2): "universal late reader fed by a pair-dependent early-write stage; contrast with IOI late name-movers" — the TWO-STAGE STRUCTURE is standard; the IOI CONTRAST is weak.**
- The early-establish/late-read division of labor is the **standard compositional template** in mech interp: induction heads require a previous-token head (early) feeding an induction head (later) (Olsson et al. 2022; Elhage et al. 2021); IOI itself is a multi-stage circuit where duplicate-token and S-inhibition heads (earlier) set up the name-mover heads (later) (Wang et al. 2023).
- The factual-recall pipeline of **Geva et al. (2023, "Dissecting Recall of Factual Associations", EMNLP 2023)** is an almost exact structural match: "the representation at the last-subject position goes through an enrichment process, driven by the early MLP sublayers" (early write), and then "the prediction representation 'queries' the enriched subject to extract the attribute" via attention heads (late read). The report's "anchored bias established in the first layer, read out mid-stack" is this same early-enrich/late-extract motif.
- The specific contrast the report draws — "unlike IOI's LATE name-movers, here the bias is established early" — is **largely a false distinction**, because IOI's name-movers are themselves fed by earlier-established signals (S-inhibition, duplicate-token heads). The relevant prior work shows the late copy head is always fed by earlier-stage information; that is the norm, not a contrast.

**Sub-claim (3): "sycophancy is a strategy, not a circuit; different cues route through different heads" — DEEPEST and most defensibly novel, but its components are each precedented.**
- **"Sycophancy is not one mechanism" is directly stated by Vennemeyer, Duong, Zhan & Jiang (2025, "Sycophancy Is Not One Thing", arXiv:2509.21305):** they decompose sycophancy into distinct, independently steerable directions and find "(1) the three behaviors are encoded along distinct linear directions in latent space; (2) each behavior can be independently amplified or suppressed without affecting the others... These results suggest that sycophantic behaviors correspond to distinct, independently steerable representations." Their steering effects "emerge first around layer 20." However, their decomposition is along *behavior types* (agreement vs. praise) and is at the level of *linear directions/subspaces*, not attention-head routing.
- **"The same task is implemented by different circuits depending on prompt subtype" is the thesis of Franco, Tassis, Rohr & Crovella (2026, "Finding Highly Interpretable Prompt-Specific Circuits in Language Models", arXiv:2602.13483):** "Applying ACC++ to indirect object identification (IOI) in GPT-2, Pythia, and Gemma 2, we find there is no single circuit for IOI in any model: different prompt templates induce systematically different mechanisms." Méloux et al. (2025) make a related non-identifiability/instability point. So "cue-dependent routing of a behavior through different heads" is an established meta-finding, including on gemma-2.
- **"Arithmetic is implemented diffusely" is established by Nikankin, Reusch, Mueller & Belinkov (2025, "Arithmetic Without Algorithms: ... Bag of Heuristics", arXiv:2410.21272, ICLR 2025):** "LLMs perform arithmetic using neither robust algorithms nor memorization; rather, they rely on a 'bag of heuristics'" implemented by "a sparse set of important neurons." Stolfo et al. (2023) explicitly contrast diffuse arithmetic processing with concentrated entity factual recall. This supports the report's claim that the numeric-assertion copy is carried by a more distributed set of heads while the entity/salience copy concentrates in L18.H5.
- **What appears genuinely novel:** No located source reports the *specific* dichotomy that a salience-framing cue routes the copy through the single concentrated reader head L18.H5 while a numeric-assertion sycophancy cue routes the same copy through a different, diffuse mid-stack set with L18.H5 carrying essentially none of the numeric copy. The pieces are precedented; this particular head-level instantiation in gemma-2-2b is not.

## Details

### A. Induction heads and token-level copying (Olsson et al. 2022; Elhage et al. 2021)
Induction heads establish the baseline that transformers contain dedicated attention heads whose OV circuit copies an attended token to the output and boosts its logit, with a QK circuit deciding where to look. Crucially, induction is a **two-head, two-stage composition**: a previous-token head writes "what came before" into the residual stream, and a later induction head reads it to complete `[A][B]...[A]→[B]`. Relationship to the described mechanism: the "attention-copy" behavior is a copy-to-output via an OV circuit, exactly the induction-head primitive — but the described mechanism is *not* prefix-matching/in-context completion. It copies a *semantically salient/asserted* token rather than the token that followed a previous match. So it is "induction-like in primitive, name-mover-like in function." The two-stage early-write/late-read structure the report highlights is precisely the induction template, so it is not novel as a structure.

### B. IOI name-movers and copy-suppression (Wang et al. 2023; McDougall et al. 2023)
This is the report's own comparison point and the closest match for sub-claim (1). Name-mover heads in GPT-2 small (notably 9.9, 9.6, 10.0) are active at END, attend to a name earlier in the prompt, and copy it to the output via a name-copying OV matrix. The salience-reader head is functionally a name-mover head whose query has been captured by a salient distractor rather than the correct indirect object. The report's attempt to distinguish itself from IOI by saying "the bias is established early rather than via late name-movers" is weak: IOI name-movers are *themselves* late heads fed by earlier S-inhibition and duplicate-token heads, so "early-established, late-read" describes IOI too.

**Copy-suppression** (McDougall et al. 2023, "Copy Suppression: Comprehensively Understanding an Attention Head", NeurIPS 2023; head L10.H7 in GPT-2) is the mirror-image phenomenon: a late head that *suppresses* a token that is being naively copied/predicted, attending back to its earlier occurrence (their algorithm explains 76.9% of L10.H7's behavior). This matters for the report's framing because the field already has a rich taxonomy of late heads that read a referenced token and either copy it (name-movers) or suppress it (copy-suppression / negative name-movers / anti-induction heads). The **backup name-mover** phenomenon (heads that take over the copy role when primary name-movers are ablated) is also directly relevant to any knockout-based methodology: knocking out L18.H5 may trigger compensatory copying by backup heads, which the report should rule out before claiming L18.H5 is *the* copy head.

The **competition-of-mechanisms** line (Ortu et al. 2024; reproductions arXiv 2506.22977 and 2507.11809) is highly relevant: it studies exactly the factual-vs-counterfactual (in-context) copy competition, finds a small set of attention heads that attend to the counterfactual "attribute" token and copy it, and finds factual-promoting heads (L10.H7, L11.H10) acting as copy regulators/suppressors. This is arguably the closest existing framing to "a referenced in-context token competing with the parametric answer and being copied to output," and the report should position against it explicitly.

### C. Mechanistic (vs behavioral) studies of sycophancy and framing
Most foundational sycophancy work is **behavioral**: Sharma et al. 2023 (Anthropic, "Towards Understanding Sycophancy", arXiv:2310.13548) and Perez et al. 2022 establish sycophancy as an RLHF-linked behavior via model-written evaluations, but do not open the model. Mechanistic work is more recent and supports sub-claim (3) only partially:
- Vennemeyer et al. 2025 ("Sycophancy Is Not One Thing", arXiv:2509.21305): distinct linear directions for sub-types, independently steerable, with effects emerging around layer 20 — directly supports "not a single faculty," but via directions, not heads, and decomposing agreement vs. praise rather than salience vs. numeric.
- Wang, Li, Yang, Zhang & Wang 2025 ("When Truth Is Overridden", arXiv:2508.02087, AAAI 2026): a logit-lens + activation-patching account (on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) finding "a two-stage emergence of sycophancy: (1) a late-layer output preference shift and (2) deeper representational divergence" (divergence around layers 16–19), and notably that *cue type matters* — opinion statements induce sycophancy while expertise/authority framing does not, and "first-person prompts ('I believe…') consistently induce higher sycophancy rates than third-person framings." This both supports "cue type changes the internal effect" and partly cuts against "many circuits," since it favors a unified two-stage override.
- There is also a Llama-3.1-8B activation-steering line (CAA / difference-of-means directions) but this is steering/representation work, not head-level circuit routing.

### D. Salience / distractor-driven attention (the thinnest axis)
Several works show salient/distractor tokens capture attention and bias outputs, supporting the *mechanism* behind a salience-reader head:
- **Yuksekgonul, Chandrasekaran, Jones, Gunasekar, Naik, Palangi, Kamar & Nushi 2024 ("Attention Satisfies", arXiv:2309.15098, ICLR 2024):** "We find a strong positive relationship between the LLM's attention to constraint tokens and the factual accuracy of generations" — i.e., where attention goes determines factual correctness — across "10 datasets containing over 40,000 prompts" on the Llama-2 family (7B, 13B, 70B), with entity popularity/salience modulating this.
- **"Racing Thoughts" (Lepori et al., arXiv:2410.02102, NAACL 2025) on Gemma-2-2b:** uses distractor and cue ablations (in blocks of 5 layers) finding that "ablating cue tokens drops performance and ablating distractors increases performance" — a direct demonstration that distractor tokens capture attention and degrade answers via early-layer contextualization, with the dependency resolved roughly halfway through the stack. This is a strong structural analogue to the report's "early-establish, mid-stack read."
- **Atlas / "Attention Speaks Volumes" (2024)** localizes bias to specific layers by analyzing last-token attention to competing entities and intervenes by scaling attention — again, attention-to-salient-entity drives biased outputs.
- **Attention-sink literature** (Xiao et al.; "Interpreting the Repeated Token Phenomenon"; "How Attention Sinks Emerge") shows a two-stage "early layer marks the token, later component acts on it" structure for position/repetition salience — structurally parallel but about positional/BOS sinks, not semantic distractors.

No located source reports a *named late head that copies a famous-distractor token to the answer slot under framing/sycophancy prompts*, so the specific salience-reader-head instantiation is not directly precedented — though it is a natural composition of name-mover behavior + distractor-attention findings.

### E. GemmaScope / gemma-2-2b circuit analyses
- **Ferrando et al. 2024 ("Do I Know This Entity?", arXiv:2411.14257, ICLR 2025 oral):** the single most overlapping source. Uses GemmaScope SAEs on gemma-2-2b/9b; names **L18.H5 and L20.H3 as "attribute extraction heads"** (Appendix L, Table 7) that move entity attributes to the final token; shows entity-recognition directions modulate these heads' attention to the entity ("disrupt the attention of downstream heads that typically move entity attributes to the final token"). This means the report's "reader head L18.H5" is already a documented attribute-extraction head, and the report's contribution is reframing it as a *salience/sycophancy copy* head and supplying the early-write feeder.
- **Anthropic's "On the Biology of a Large Language Model" / circuit-tracing (2025):** uses cross-layer transcoders and attribution graphs (and explicitly demonstrates on gemma-2-2b for some analyses); their "capital of the state containing Dallas" example is the canonical entity→attribute copy attribution graph, and their "known entity" features inhibiting a default refusal circuit closely mirror Ferrando et al. This is the methodological lineage the report's transcoder/knockout approach sits in.
- Misc GemmaScope attention-feature explorations (e.g., LessWrong "Attention-Feature Tables in Gemma 2 Residual Streams") exist but are exploratory and do not report the specific heads/circuits.

## Recommendations
1. **Reframe sub-claim (1) as a re-identification plus extension, not a discovery.** State explicitly that L18.H5 was named an "attribute extraction head" by Ferrando et al. (2024, Appendix L, Table 7), and position the contribution as showing this *same* head is co-opted under salience/framing prompts to copy a *distractor* (rather than the correct attribute) to the output. This is honest and still interesting. Benchmark that would change this: if the report can show L18.H5's behavior under framing is mechanistically distinct from attribute extraction (different QK source, different OV target class), the novelty claim strengthens.
2. **Drop or heavily qualify the "early-established vs late name-mover" IOI distinction.** It does not survive contact with the IOI circuit (S-inhibition/duplicate-token heads feed late name-movers) or Geva et al.'s early-MLP-enrichment/late-attention-extraction pipeline. Replace it with a sharper, defensible contrast: e.g., that the *feeder* signal here is a salience/anchor signal rather than a duplicate-token/position signal.
3. **Make sub-claim (3) the headline novelty, and cite the three precedents directly.** Position against Vennemeyer et al. 2025 (sycophancy = multiple directions), Franco et al. 2026 (prompt-specific circuits: "no single circuit for IOI"), and Nikankin et al. 2025 (arithmetic = bag of heuristics). The defensible new result is the *head-level* routing dichotomy (concentrated L18.H5 for salience vs. diffuse mid-stack for numeric) — emphasize that prior work showed this at the level of directions/subspaces or for IOI templates, not for sycophancy copy routing in gemma-2-2b.
4. **Rule out backup/compensatory copying in the knockout analysis.** Given the well-documented backup-name-mover/self-repair phenomenon (Wang et al. 2023; McDougall et al. 2023), test whether knocking out L18.H5 triggers compensation before claiming it is the unique salience copy head.
5. **Engage the competition-of-mechanisms line (Ortu et al. 2024) explicitly**, since it is the closest existing treatment of "in-context referenced token copied to output in competition with the parametric answer," including copy-regulator heads.

## Caveats
- I did not have access to the underlying project report itself (it was not located in the connected Drive, which contained related but distinct sycophancy-steering project documents — e.g., a Llama-3.1-8B steering-target-atoms monograph and "SycophantSee" prompt-time diagnostics). My assessment is based on the mechanism as described in the query; if the report's actual claims are narrower or more specific, some "precedented" judgments may soften.
- Several key supporting sources are recent arXiv preprints not yet peer-reviewed (notably Franco et al. 2026 on prompt-specific circuits; Méloux et al. 2025 on non-identifiability; some 2025–2026 sycophancy mechanistic papers). Treat their findings as suggestive rather than settled. Peer-reviewed anchors include Olsson et al. 2022, Wang et al. 2023, McDougall et al. 2023 (NeurIPS), Geva et al. 2023 (EMNLP), Yuksekgonul et al. 2024 (ICLR), Ferrando et al. (ICLR 2025 oral), Nikankin et al. (ICLR 2025), and Wang et al. ("When Truth Is Overridden", AAAI 2026).
- "Novel" here means "no direct prior report found in the venues searched," not a guarantee of absolute novelty; the mech interp literature is large and fast-moving, and attention-feature/Neuronpedia explorations are hard to search exhaustively.
- The match between L18.H5-as-attribute-extraction-head (Ferrando) and L18.H5-as-salience-reader-head (the report) is based on head-index identity and the shared "moves entity attributes to the final token" description; I could not verify from primary sources that the two analyses examine identical query/value behavior, only that both implicate the same head in entity-attribute movement to the final token.
