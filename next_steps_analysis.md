# Next Steps for the Attention-Copy Mechanism Analysis

Before listing tests, the single most consequential finding from the research bears directly on the base-vs-chat question, so it leads.

## The base-vs-chat distinction is doing more work than the repo currently credits

The repo already has the right instinct here — §6 flags the base-fragment regime as "the largest confound," §8 shows RLHF deletes the reader head's attention to the anchor from the weights (0.84→0.01 on the *identical* prompt), and §10.2 finds salience and numeric copy route through different heads. But the literature reframes what that base-vs-chat gap *means*, and it's not flattering to the headline.

The problem: **the most legible, most generalising result — the concentrated L18.H5 salience copy — lives almost entirely in the regime nobody deploys.** §8 shows it's gone in the it-model's weights; §6 shows a QA scaffold disengages it even in base. Meanwhile the result that *survives* into the it-model — numeric authority-sycophancy, which still flips when the model can't self-verify (§9) — is carried by the *diffuse* mid-stack heads that are understood *least*. So there's an inverse relationship between how well something has been mechanised and how much it matters for real usage. That's worth stating plainly rather than letting the clean L18.H5 story carry the paper.

Two framings of the base-vs-chat gap, and they have different implications:

- **"RLHF removes the mechanism"** (the §8 reading): the copy circuit is a base-model artifact, trained away. If true, the interesting science is the *training dynamics* — when does L18.H5 stop attending to anchors over RLHF? — and the deployed-relevance is mostly the numeric residue.
- **"RLHF masks/reroutes the mechanism"**: the copy strategy persists but gets gated or redistributed. The Wang et al. "When Truth Is Overridden" result (sycophancy as a late-layer override emerging ~layers 16–19 in instruct models) is closer to *masking* than deletion, and it cuts mildly against the clean "deleted from weights" claim. Distinguishing deletion from masking is the highest-value open question, and it's testable.

## Tests, roughly in priority order

**1. Deletion vs. masking on the it-model (highest value).** §8 measures L18.H5's *attention to the anchor* and finds it collapsed. But low attention isn't the same as an absent capability. Test whether the copy can be *restored* under intervention: patch the base-model's L18.H5 attention pattern (or its QK) into the it-model on the same prompt and see if the flip returns. If a single attention-pattern transplant resurrects the copy, the OV machinery survived and RLHF only changed where the head looks (masking/QK-gating); if it doesn't, the OV pathway itself degraded (closer to deletion). This directly adjudicates the §8 claim and is the most defensible novel result available.

**2. Address the backup/self-repair confound before any "L18.H5 is *the* head" claim.** This is the most important *correction*, not just an addition. Arc 1 already rediscovered the Hydra effect (§1.4, L19/7477 reading negative). The name-mover literature (Wang et al.; McDougall et al.) documents backup name-movers that take over when the primary is ablated. The §3.10 single-head necessities (L18.H5 ~0.2 alone, ~12 heads to reach ~0.94) are *consistent with* heavy redundancy. Run the knockout-then-remeasure: ablate L18.H5 and check whether other heads' attention-to-anchor *increases*. If it does, "concentrated reader head" overstates it — it's a primary among backups, which is a different and more honest claim.

**3. Disambiguate L18.H5 from the Ferrando "attribute extraction head."** This is the sharpest novelty test the research surfaced. Ferrando et al. already named L18.H5 an attribute-extraction head that moves entity attributes to the final token. The contribution only holds if the *framing/salience* behavior is mechanistically distinct from ordinary attribute extraction — not just the same head doing its normal job pointed at a distractor. Concretely: compare the QK source and the OV target-class on (a) clean attribute extraction ("capital of Australia is" → Canberra) vs (b) the framed copy ("Sydney is famous… is" → Sydney). If QK source and OV write are the same machinery and only the *query's winning key* differs, then the mechanism is "a known head gets captured by a salient distractor" — still a real result, but a smaller and more accurate one than "we found a salience copy head."

**4. Forward-patching the early-write stage.** §3.10 says the early writer is pair-dependent (L0.H2 Australia-specific). That's currently the weakest-characterised stage and it's where the "early-established bias" novelty claim was supposed to live — except the research shows early-write/late-read is the *standard* template (induction; Geva's early-MLP-enrich/late-attention-extract). Test whether the early writer is even an attention head or whether MLP enrichment does the work (Geva-style): zero early MLPs at the anchor position and see if L18.H5 still finds the anchor. If MLPs carry it, "early-write head" is the wrong description.

**5. Competition-of-mechanisms positioning, made empirical.** Ortu et al. study exactly this structure: an in-context referenced token copied to output in competition with the parametric answer, with copy-*regulator* heads. The confidence→susceptibility law (low baseline confidence flips, high holds) is plausibly the *balance* between a copy head and a suppression/regulator head. Look for the mirror head — a copy-suppression/negative name-mover that defends Canberra — and test whether susceptibility tracks the *ratio* of copy-head to suppressor-head strength rather than copy-head strength alone. This would convert the descriptive confidence law into a mechanistic one.

**6. Generalise off the gemma family before any scaling claim.** §10.1 already retracted a cross-size log-prob trend as not-comparable, and the "9b-it is robust" confound (no low-confidence item to flip) is a clean catch. The honest move is one cross-architecture replication (a Pythia or GPT-2-family model) of just the routing dichotomy in §10.2 — does salience still concentrate and numeric still diffuse? Franco et al. ("no single circuit for IOI in any model") predicts the *specific heads* won't transport but the *routing pattern* might. That's the claim worth testing, and it's narrower than what §10 currently gestures at.

## What to actually prioritise

If the goal is the strongest defensible paper: **tests 2 and 3 are corrective and should come first** — they protect against overclaiming on the result the literature most directly challenges (L18.H5 is already named; single-head necessity is low). **Test 1 is the highest-upside new result** — deletion-vs-masking is genuinely open and directly deployment-relevant. **Test 5 gives the cleanest novel mechanistic story** (the routing dichotomy reframed as cue-dependent copy-vs-regulator balance), which is also the axis the research flagged as the thinnest, most-defensible novelty.

On the base-vs-chat question specifically, the honest read: the repo should stop treating the base-model regime as a *limitation to apologise for* and start treating the base-vs-chat *difference itself* as the object of study. The mechanism is clearest in base, deployment-relevant in chat, and the transformation between them (test 1) is both the most novel and the most useful thing here. The clean L18.H5 salience story is, somewhat awkwardly, the part that matters least.

One caveat on all of the above: this reasons from the summary's reported numbers, not the artifacts or code. Several of these tests (especially 2 and 4) could be partly answered by data already in the committed JSONs — worth checking before running anything new.
